How New York Paycheck Taxes Work in 2026

Every New York worker's paycheck is reduced by up to four layers of tax: federal income tax, FICA (Social Security + Medicare), New York State income tax, and — if you live in New York City or Yonkers — a local tax on top. Here's exactly what each layer is.

1. Federal Income Tax (7 Brackets, 10%–37%)

Federal tax is the largest deduction for most workers. It uses seven progressive brackets — you only pay each rate on the income within that bracket, not on your total income. The 2026 standard deduction is $16,100 (single), $32,200 (MFJ), and $24,150 (HOH), set by IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32.

2. FICA: Social Security & Medicare

FICA taxes are flat-rate and taken from nearly every paycheck regardless of state. For 2026:

  • Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $184,500 (confirmed by SSA.gov, up from $176,100 in 2025)
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all wages with no cap
  • Additional Medicare Tax: +0.9% on wages above $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ)

The bottom rate dropped from 4% to 3.9%, and the middle bracket dropped from 6% to 5.9%. The high-income surcharge brackets (9.65%, 10.3%, 10.9%) remain in effect through 2032.

4. NYC City Tax & Yonkers Surcharge

NYC residents pay an additional city income tax of 3.078% to 3.876%. Yonkers residents pay a surcharge equal to 16.75% of their NY state tax liability. Neither tax applies to residents of the rest of New York State.

5. NY Paid Family Leave (PFL) & State Disability (SDI)

These small but mandatory deductions fund NY's leave programs. For 2026: PFL is 0.432% of gross wages (max $411.91/year), up from 0.388% in 2025. SDI remains at $0.60/week (max $31.20/year).

Frequently Asked Questions

What changed in New York taxes for 2026? ▾
Several things changed for 2026: (1) New York reduced its five lowest income tax brackets under Chapter 59 of the Laws of 2025 — the bottom rate dropped from 4% to 3.9%. (2) The NY PFL rate increased from 0.388% to 0.432% with a higher max of $411.91/year. (3) The Social Security wage base rose from $176,100 to $184,500. (4) Federal standard deductions increased to $16,100 (single) and $32,200 (MFJ) under IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32.
How much is taken out of a $70,000 salary in New York City? ▾
For a single NYC resident earning $70,000/year paid bi-weekly in 2026 (standard deductions, no pre-tax deductions): Federal tax ~$7,450, Social Security ~$4,340, Medicare ~$1,015, NY State tax ~$3,250, NYC tax ~$2,480, NY PFL ~$302, NY SDI ~$31. Total deductions ~$18,870. Annual net take-home ~$51,130, or about $1,966/bi-weekly paycheck. Use our calculator above for your exact figures.
I work in NYC but live in New Jersey. Do I pay NYC tax? ▾
No — NYC city tax only applies to NYC residents, not non-residents who work there. If you live in NJ but work in NYC, you pay NY State income tax on your NY wages, but not NYC city tax. You would also owe NJ state income tax on your income. NJ and NY do not have a reciprocity agreement, so you'd typically file in both states and receive a credit in NJ for taxes paid to NY.
Does contributing to a 401(k) reduce my New York taxes? ▾
Yes. Traditional 401(k) contributions reduce both your federal taxable income and your NY state taxable income. The 2026 401(k) contribution limit is $23,500 ($31,000 if age 50+). Enter your per-paycheck 401(k) contribution in the "Pre-Tax Deductions" field to see the tax savings. A $500/month contribution could save an NYC worker $2,000–$3,500+ in combined taxes annually depending on their bracket.
Is there a calculator for freelancers / 1099 workers in New York? ▾
This calculator is designed for W-2 employees. Self-employed / 1099 workers in New York face different rules: you pay self-employment tax (15.3% = both employer and employee FICA), no NY PFL deduction applies, and you must make quarterly estimated tax payments. NY also has no SDI deduction for self-employed. Key difference: 1099 workers can deduct the employer-half of SE tax (7.65%) from gross income. A dedicated freelance tax calculator or CPA is recommended for accurate estimates.
Does New York tax Social Security income? ▾
No. New York State does not tax Social Security benefits, which is a meaningful advantage for retirees compared to states that do tax them. NY also partially exempts pension and annuity income — taxpayers can exclude up to $20,000 of eligible pension/annuity income if they meet certain age and income requirements.
